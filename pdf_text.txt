1. Introduction  
According to the World Health Organization (WHO), almost the entire population of the planet, or 99%, inhales 
air that contains higher -than -recommended levels of certain pollutants, and thus, air pollution becomes a global 
health risk of the first order. T his situation is extremely serious in large cities, like Delhi, as it results in a significant 
increase in the number of cases of respiratory and cardiovascular diseases.  Most air quality index (AQI) apps show 
city-wide average data from a small number of official monitoring stations. This method doesn't take into account 
the small, street -level differences in pollution or how an individual's risk of exposure changes based on how they 
move. This paper presents a system designed to fill this gap by forecasting hyper -local air quality and offering 
health -conscious navigation.

1.1  Background and Motivation  
The fast growth of cities has led to environmental problems that have never been seen before. One of the biggest 
problems to public health is air pollution. The Forum of International Respiratory Societies says that air pollution 
is a major cause of many no ncommunicable diseases, which kill millions of people too soon each year [15 ]. In 
Delhi, levels of particulate matter (𝑃𝑀2.5) often go well above safe levels, which has serious health effects for the 
people who live there.  
1.2  Problem Statement and Research Gap  
A small network of stationary monitoring stations is the foundation of current air quality management techniques.  
The small spatial resolution of the data is extremely important for citywide alerts. This ends up creating a large 
information gap since a person's real exposure to pollutants is very variable and influenced by their exact location 
and movement through dif ferent urban microhabitats [1]. The citywide data becomes unsuitable for individual 
health risk management due to the difference between the reported ambient air quality and the actual personal 
exposure.  
1.3 Proposed Solution and Approach  
The present study suggests a framework for predicting human mobility patterns and mapping the associated air 
quality exposure risk that will help to eliminate the existing gap. The proposed method offers a dynamic and 
predictive model that recognizes the c omplex interconnections between meteorology, urban mobility, and the 
movement of pollutants. Thanks to the merging of real -time data streams with a potent machine learning model, 
the result is a highly localized, instant pollution map of the city’s transpo rtation system.  
1.4 Key Contribution s 
This work makes three main contributions:  
1. Hybrid Spatiotemporal Prediction Model: To make accurate predictions of 𝑃𝑀2.5 concentrations for 
non-monitored locations, a machine learning model is developed that implements advanced feature 
engineering techniques that take into account the distance to significant traffic routes and cyclical time 
patterns.  
2. A Dynamic Exposure -Aware Routing Algorithm:  To enable a straight comparison of various travel 
alternatives, we created an algorithm that uses these hyper -local predictions and measures the pollution 
exposure along a particular route accumulatively.  
3.  A Functional Prototype and Case Study: The entire application was developed as a web app for Delhi, 
proving its effectiveness by allowing the users to select between the fastest route and one that was 
obviously less polluted.

2. Related Work  
The three main areas of our work are health -oriented routing, personal exposure assessment, and air quality (AQ) 
prediction . 
When compared to traditional statistical techniques, AQ prediction has been greatly improved by developments 
in machine learning (ML ) [11].  Because they could model intricate, non -linear relationships between predictors 
like weather and pollution levels, models like Random Forests (RF), which were used in early machine learning 
applications, were successful for spatiotemporal 𝑃𝑀2.5 prediction, [17].  In recent years, gradient boosting 
frameworks —in particular, XGBoost —have frequently become the preferred model due to their superior 
accuracy and robustness in comparative studies [16], [3]  At the same time, deep learning has become increasingly 
popular in the field.  Architectures like Long Short -Term Memory (LSTM) networks, hybrid Convolutional -LSTM 
models [12], [7], and Deep SpatioTemporal Residual Networks (ST -ResNets) [18] are establishi ng new standards 
in city -wide AQ forecasting by automatically learning complex spatial and temporal dependencies. To improve 
hyper -local accuracy, our work introduces new, context -aware geographic features while utilizing the high 
performance of tree -base d ensemble models.

Even though these prediction models show a high degree of accuracy, they are not very useful if they are 
not applied to a person's actual experiences. The data from sparse, fixed monitoring stations, which are 
insufficient to capture an individual's actual  pollution intake during daily transit, were the basis for traditional 
environmental health assessments. Mobility patterns are important factors that influence an individual's overall 
pollution intake, as evidenced by the groundbreaking work of De Nazelle et al. [4], who showed how GPS data 
could produce high -resolution personal exposure profiles. Later studies have confirmed the importance of this 
dynamic assessment method [14]  and the specific application of mobile phone data  [5]. Additionally, Chen et al. 
[2] made a quantification of this impact in actual working conditions, and they validated that by using different 
paths, even between the same starting and ending points, one can obtain very different exposure levels. The 
outcome of this research puts em phasis on the urgent requirement for a mobility - centred strategy, which in turn 
acts as the prime motivator of our system.  
The invention of "eco -routing" systems that take into account factors other than time and distance is the next 
step of this research [6]. A technique called "health -oriented routing" that aims to find the routes that minimize a 
commuter’s exposure to pollu tants, completely changes the focus from vehicle efficiency to human well -being. To 
approximate pollution levels between isolated monitoring stations, early systems such as the one suggested by 
Zheng et al.  [19] often depended on basic spatial interpolati on using real -time AQ data. Due to the fact that it 
neglects the complex, microenvironmental factors that make up pollution's fine -grained variation, this technique 
might not be accurate. Our main contribution and differentiating factor is the development of a high -resolution, 
dynamic pollution surface across the entire road network using a predictive machine learning model. This 
represents a considerable methodological advance over earlier studies, and it more accurately and finely 
calcula tes route -based exposure risk [13], [9]. Our system not only brings together the areas of personalized, 
actionable public health recommendations and predictive environmental modelling but also combines these three 
research areas.

4.2 User Interface and Interaction Flow  
The interface of this application has been designed with great attention so that users will be able to interact easily

is a Delhi map that users  can interact with and that offers them the opportunity to:  
1) Visualize Real -time Pollution Heatmap: The 𝑃𝑀2.5 heatmap from our prediction model is maintained 
dynamically on the map. The heatmap is visualized through  the road network and urban areas by the 
hyper -local  air quality with regions of high (red/orange) and low  (green/blue) pollution concentrations. 
The determination  of the air quality spatial variation is made very easy for  the users even before they 
plan their travels due to this  visual component which is so important.  
2) Define Origin and Destination:  By clicking on particular points on the interactive map or by entering 
addresses  into designated text fields, users can enter their start and  end locations.  
3) Request Routes:  When one clicks the "Find Best  Routes" button, the application connects 
asynchronously  to the sorting machine in the backend.  
4) Visualize and Compare Routes:  And, the system also  superimposes the two main routes on the real -
time  pollution heatmap.  
• Fastest Route:  Calculated through the Google Maps  Directions API and fine -tuned for the least 
possible  travel time considering the traffic condition at that  particular moment. Generally, this route 
is displayed  in a certain color, for example, blue.  
• Cleanest Route:  Identified by our dynamic routing  technique as having the lowest expected 
cumulative 𝑃𝑀2.5 exposure. This route’s contrasting color  (green, for example) clearly illustrates how 
it might  avoid high -pollution  areas that are visible on the  heatmap underneath.   
Tooltip or overlay, which are easy to see, are  connected to each visualized route and they show  the 
route type  (Fastest/Cleanest), the travel time  estimated, and the average 𝑃𝑀2.5 exposure score.  By 
utilizing the pollution heatmap, a temporary comparison is made, which is the main reason for users' 
insights that can be easily applied to making health -preferring travel choices.

5.4 Discussion and System Implications  
A robust visual connection (Fig. 2) and a minimal Mean Absolute Error (Table II) reaffirmed the strong ability 
of the model to accurately forecast 𝑃𝑀2.5 at the non -monitoring locations, thus paving the way for a new kind of 
personalized environmental services. The trip from Connaught Place to Dwarka demonstrated that 𝑃𝑀2.5 
exposure was considerably and notably lowered by 27.3% with an increase in travel time of only 10 minutes. This 
is the transformation of urban dwellers into an active preventive measure that relies on the conversion of passive 
environmental data into healt h-oriented choices, thus empowering them.  
The model's interpretability is very important for revealing the meaning of this finding. The SHAP analysis 
(Fig. 4) not only shows the correlation but also reveals the causal -like insights that support our feature 
engineering as a perfect depiction of the  physics and traffic dynamics of urban pollution. A belief of the model in 
factors like congested roads and closeness to main highways as having a major influence on pollution level is what 
makes it possible for the system to suggest a route with less poll ution.  The system indeed not only informs about 
the present -air-quality but also gives practical advice concerning the risk of personal exposure.  
Furthermore, the modular architecture that is built on commercial -grade  APIs guarantees the scalability and 
durability of the system for use in actual  urban areas. The choice of a high -performance model such as XGBoost 
guaran tees that real -time predictions can be done with very little delay, which is a key  requirement for dynamic 
routing applications where speed is the main aspect.  Setting up a database and having automated data collection 
pipelines in place  are the two main factors that support the growing  of the data and the continued  maintenance 
of the system.  
5.5 Limitatio n 
Although the current prototype has potential, there are still some weaknesses that are going to be mentioned and 
that might be useful in future research.  
1. Pollutant Scope: The model is mainly focusing on 𝑃𝑀2.5 since it is working  with a vast amount 
of data and hence it is very  much accessible. However,  extending the prediction to 𝑃𝑀10, carbon 
monoxide (CO), ozone (O3), and  nitrogen dioxide (NO2) that are also significant pollutants would 
provide a  more thorough exposure assessment.  
2. Emission Source Detail:  Although the real_time_traffic_index is a very  good substitute for it, the 
model does not distinguish emissions sources, for  example, industrial stack emissions, 
construction dust, residential heating,  and different vehicle types, explicitly. Predicting more 
accurately could be  done by including more detailed land -use data, comprehensive emission 
inventories, or even mobile sensors’ data through the identification of specific  contributors.  
3. Micro -Environmental Factors:  Highly localized microenvironmental ef fects that can have a 
substantial impact on pollution dispersion at a very  fine spatial scale, such as building wake 
effects, the presence of green infras tructure (parks and trees), and urban canyon geometry 
(narrow streets be tween tall buildings), are not explicitly taken into account by the model. The  
aforementioned issue can be rectified by employing LiDAR -sourced dataor elaborate 3D models 
of the urban landscape.  
4. Prediction Horizon: Real -time prediction for instantaneous routing is the  main focus of the 
current system. One of the benefits that would be de rived from the establishment of robust 
forecasting abilities for distant future  periods (e.g., estimating pollution for a trip set to begin in 
three hours)  is that it would open the way for a more advanced and tactical approach  to planning. 
It would probably be necessary to incorporate more weather  forecasting models for this.

6. Conclusion and Future Work  
6.1 Conclusion  
This study introduced a brand -new, integrated system for exposure risk mapping and human mobility -aware air 
quality prediction that is intended for urban  settings like Delhi. The crucial discrepancy between coarse, citywide 
air quality  data and the real, dynamic pollution exposure people experience on their daily  commutes was 
effectively closed. We showed how to forecast air pollution at a  hyper -local level throughout the urban road 
network by designing a full -stack  application that combines a spatiotempora l XGBoost model with sophisticated  
feature engineering, such as real -time traffic indices and geographic context.  
The conversion of these predictions into a useful, health -focused navigation  tool is the main contribution of 
this work. Our case study unequivocally demon strated that the system can find alternate routes that, for a slight 
increase in  travel time, provide a significant reduction in commuter 𝑃𝑀2.5 exposure. Through our steps, we 
demonstrate a clear intention of giving the community data -based tools which would help them to select the 
healthier way of living and thus by exposing the air pollution risk that was previously unnoticed, establishing and 
encouraging  a more  eco-friendly and health -oriented city living area.  
6.2 Future Work   
Our prototype, although very promising, has led us to discover several interesting research and development 
pathways that can be explored to further enhance its effectiveness and penetration:  
1. Advanced Deep Learning Models: Our plan for the future is to explore more sophisticated deep 
learning models such as LSTMs coupled with Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs). The GCN, 
by mapping the road network graphically, might have a superior grasp of the pollution diffusion 
through t he transport system which would result in a greater prediction accuracy.  
2. Expanded Pollutant Scope: 𝑃𝑀2.5 is the primary pollutant considered in the present model. In 
the coming years, the framework will be broadened to predict a wider range of harmful 
pollutants, namely, carbon monoxide (CO), ozone (O3), and nitrogen dioxide (NO2), thereby 
offering a more deta iled and comprehensive exposure risk assessment.  
3. Integration of Micro -Environmental Data: To enhance the hyper -local precision, we will 
consider including additional data sources with more details. The study makes use of a number 
of methods, one of which is land -use data, to assess the impact of the presence of greenery like 
parks, additionally , the cityscape is modeled in 3D or LiDAR data is used to analyze the 
configuration of buildings and the effects of urban canyons.  
4. Development of a Mobile Application and User Study: We shall also create a mobile app 
specifically designed for both iOS and Android operating systems, which will ultimately increase 
the deployment and impact in the real world. Then, a user study will later on be carried out with 
the purpose of assessing th e system's user -friendliness, its effect on commuter behavior, and the 
extent of exposure reduction.  
5. Personalized Exposure Modeling:  The system upgrade might provide a chance to present users 
with more personalized risk profiles. This would mean allowing users to select their preferred 
transport mode (e.g., enclosed car, scooter, pedestrian, cyclist), as different transport modes lead 
to different levels of exposure even on the same route.
